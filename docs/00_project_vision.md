\---



\## 1. Purpose



ARGOS is a long-term engineering project created to bridge the gap between embedded systems, robotics, electronics and autonomous systems.



Rather than developing isolated demonstrations or one-time academic projects, ARGOS is designed as a continuously evolving engineering platform where every generation introduces new capabilities while preserving compatibility with previous work.



The project represents a complete engineering journey, beginning with the fundamentals of robotic motion and eventually expanding toward autonomous perception, intelligent decision making and reliable real-world operation.



\---



\## 2. Vision



The long-term vision of ARGOS is to become a fully autonomous indoor robotic platform capable of monitoring its environment, executing missions and interacting with a larger smart infrastructure without continuous human intervention.



The robot should eventually be capable of:



\- Navigating autonomously in indoor environments.

\- Understanding its own position.

\- Detecting obstacles and avoiding collisions.

\- Receiving missions remotely.

\- Reporting system health continuously.

\- Returning to a charging station when necessary.

\- Acting as an autonomous indoor security and observation platform.



Rather than being optimized for a single demonstration, ARGOS is intended to become a reusable robotics platform on which future ideas can be implemented and tested.



\---



\## 3. Mission



The mission of ARGOS is not simply to build a robot.



Its mission is to create a professional engineering platform capable of demonstrating practical knowledge in:



\- Embedded Systems

\- Robotics

\- Electronics Design

\- PCB Design

\- Power Electronics

\- Motor Control

\- Sensor Fusion

\- Autonomous Navigation

\- Software Architecture

\- Communication Systems

\- Product Development

\- System Engineering



Every subsystem developed for ARGOS should contribute to at least one of these engineering disciplines.



\---



\## 4. Engineering Philosophy



ARGOS follows several fundamental engineering principles.



\### 4.1 Validate Before Designing



Every subsystem will first be implemented using development boards and modular components.



Only after successful validation will custom hardware be designed.



This approach reduces unnecessary PCB revisions while ensuring that engineering decisions are supported by experimental data.



\---



\### 4.2 Incremental Development



ARGOS will evolve through small, measurable milestones.



Each completed phase must leave the robot in a fully operational state before new features are introduced.



Large architectural redesigns should be avoided whenever possible.



\---



\### 4.3 Modularity



Every subsystem should operate independently whenever practical.



Examples include:



\- Motor control

\- Sensor interfaces

\- Communication

\- Power management

\- Navigation



This modular approach allows individual components to be upgraded without redesigning the entire robot.



\---



\### 4.4 Documentation First



Engineering decisions are valuable only if they are documented.



Every important decision must answer three questions:



\- Why was this decision made?

\- Which alternatives were considered?

\- What engineering trade-offs were accepted?



Future revisions should always be understandable by reading the documentation alone.



\---



\### 4.5 Reliability Over Complexity



A reliable robot with fewer features is preferred over a complex robot that behaves unpredictably.



Every new capability must increase reliability instead of reducing it.



\---



\## 5. Design Objectives



ARGOS should be designed according to the following objectives.



\### Mechanical Objectives



\- Compact indoor platform

\- Approximately shoe-box sized

\- Easy to maintain

\- Modular mechanical assembly

\- Future compatibility with self-balancing architecture



\---



\### Electrical Objectives



\- STM32-based control system

\- Professional power distribution

\- Modular PCB architecture

\- Reliable motor control

\- Expandable sensor interfaces



\---



\### Software Objectives



\- Well-structured firmware

\- Modular software architecture

\- Hardware abstraction

\- Continuous testing

\- Future compatibility with RTOS



\---



\### Robotics Objectives



\- Stable differential drive

\- Accurate wheel odometry

\- Robust PID control

\- Reliable obstacle detection

\- Autonomous navigation

\- Future sensor fusion



\---



\## 6. Development Strategy



The project is divided into multiple engineering phases.



Each phase has clearly defined objectives, validation procedures and success criteria.



No phase should begin before the previous phase has been successfully validated.



This development strategy mirrors professional industrial product development methodologies rather than academic prototype development.



\---



\## 7. Long-Term Roadmap



The long-term evolution of ARGOS is planned as follows.



\### Generation 1

\- Encoder feedback

\- PID control



\### Generation 2



\- Position estimation

\- Sensor integration

\- Environment perception



\### Generation 3



\- Autonomous navigation

\- Obstacle avoidance



\### Generation 4



\- Custom electronics

\### Generation 5



\- Wireless telemetry

\- Home server integration

\- Remote monitoring



\### Generation 6



\- Indoor autonomous security robot



\### Generation 7



\- Advanced perception

\- Docking station

\---



\## 8. Success Criteria



ARGOS will be considered successful when it demonstrates not only a functioning robotic platform but also professional engineering practices throughout its entire development lifecycle.



Success is measured by:



\- Robust hardware design

\- Reliable software architecture

\- Thorough documentation

\- Repeatable testing

\- Incremental improvements

\- Long-term maintainability



The final outcome should represent a complete engineering portfolio rather than a single robotics project.



\---



\## 9. Final Statement



ARGOS is more than a robot.



It is a long-term engineering platform designed to continuously expand technical knowledge while demonstrating professional product development practices.



Every experiment, every failure and every improvement becomes part of the project's engineering history.



The goal is not merely to reach the final robot, but to document and understand every engineering decision made along the way.
